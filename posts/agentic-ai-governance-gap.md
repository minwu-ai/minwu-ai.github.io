As multi-step AI agents move from demos into production deployments, many organizations are discovering an uncomfortable truth: their existing model risk frameworks were designed for a fundamentally different kind of system.

Large language models, in their original form, were reactive. They received a prompt and returned a response. Governance frameworks built around established model-risk logic adapted reasonably well to this. Validation teams could evaluate inputs, outputs, and the behavior in between.

## Where the model breaks

Agentic systems break this picture. An agent that can browse the web, write and run code, manage files, and chain dozens of tool calls in a single session is not just generating text — it is taking actions in the world.

The boundaries between "model" and "system" dissolve. Errors compound across steps. Human oversight becomes a checkpoint rather than a continuous loop.

## What to do about it

For teams deploying agents today, the practical implication is simple to state and harder to execute: governance needs to catch up before risk does. That means rethinking model inventories, validation methods, and monitoring for systems that act rather than merely predict.
