## The Shift That Actually Matters

Three weeks of improvised export-control diplomacy produced something no AI safety conference has managed in three years: a public, simultaneous call from Google DeepMind, OpenAI, and Anthropic for mandatory pre-release oversight of frontier models. Hassabis laid out his plan in a personal manifesto arguing to Axios that the time has come for a "systematic" approach — industry-funded, expert-staffed, and answerable to the US government. OpenAI's Sam Altman had separately called for a similar body in a Financial Times article. Anthropic CEO Dario Amodei has issued his own call for binding regulation, envisioning an FAA-style agency. The lab chiefs behind Gemini and Claude now agree Washington should regulate them, differing mainly on who holds the authority.

That convergence is the analytical story. The institutional design details matter less than the political signal: the labs are now pulling *toward* regulation, not fighting it.

## What Hassabis Actually Proposed

The proposal is modeled on FINRA — the private, industry-funded watchdog that polices Wall Street under SEC oversight — with frontier labs initially sharing models voluntarily, up to 30 days before release, for safety testing that probes dangerous cyber, biological, and "deception" capabilities. Once the testing regime proves "effective and robust," formalization "could quickly follow."

Three structural details are operationally significant:

- **Scope by capability, not company.** Rules would apply to all frontier-class models, "no matter their country of origin or whether they are open or closed."
- **Governance structure.** A majority-independent board stacked with Turing Award winners and credentialed experts, alongside industry, government, and open-source representatives.
- **Slowdown authority.** The body "could be ratcheted up if the seriousness of the situation demands, including coordinating a slowdown in development among the Frontier Labs if deemed necessary."

Hassabis said he wants the body operational before year-end.

## The Wake-Up Call He Named

The Trump administration's improvised crackdown on Anthropic's Mythos and Fable models was "a bit of a wake-up call" — proof Washington needs something sturdier than ad hoc directives. Anthropic spent 2½ weeks negotiating model releases with no established rules or playbook. OpenAI agreed to restrict GPT-5.6 to government-vetted partners at launch, then released it publicly after negotiations with the Commerce Department.

As documented in [The Government Just Killed Two Frontier Models Overnight](https://minwu-ai.github.io/the-government-just-killed-two-frontier-models-overnight-and/) and [After the Shutdown: What Fable 5's Restoration Actually Settled](https://minwu-ai.github.io/after-the-shutdown-what-fable-5-s-restoration-actually-settl/), this episode demonstrated that a de facto preclearance regime already exists — operating through informal government pressure with no legal framework, no transparency, and no technical credibility. Hassabis's proposal is a bid to formalize and professionalize what Washington is already doing by improvisation.

## The FINRA Analogy: Where It Holds and Where It Breaks

| Dimension | FINRA (Securities) | Proposed AI-SRO |
|---|---|---|
| Legal basis | Maloney Act (1938), Exchange Act §15A | No existing statute |
| Membership trigger | Market participation | Frontier capability threshold |
| Oversight agency | SEC (existing) | No designated agency |
| Enforcement mechanism | Fines, license revocation | Unclear |
| Funding conflict | Industry-funded; criticized for capture | Same risk, arguably higher |

The SRO concept traces back to the Securities Exchange Act of 1934; the Maloney Act of 1938 then allowed broker-dealer associations to register with the SEC. That took two acts of Congress and a pre-existing regulatory agency. An AI-SRO starts with neither.

Binding market-access gates cannot be created by industry alone; they require statutory authority tied to something the government controls. White House AI advisor Sriram Krishnan has already discounted the possibility, saying "there will not be an FDA for AI." The FINRA framing attempts to thread that needle — a private SRO is not a government agency. But the industry-capture risk cannot be dismissed: a body funded "mostly by industry" that also defines which models are frontier-class and can coordinate a slowdown concentrates significant power on a structurally conflicted funding base. The [Trump AI Executive Order's voluntary 30-day window](https://minwu-ai.github.io/trump-s-ai-executive-order-builds-the-scaffold-but-won-t-lig/) already showed that voluntary pre-release review cannot bind a developer who simply declines.
