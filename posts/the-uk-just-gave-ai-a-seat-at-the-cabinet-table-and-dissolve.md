## 🏛️ What Actually Changed

The reorganization is more complex than a simple elevation. DSIT's digital responsibilities moved to a renamed Department for Digital, Culture, Media and Sport, while the Cabinet Office absorbed AI adoption in the civil service, AI strategy, and the AI Security Institute — with a new AI taskforce established inside a freshly created Office for the Prime Minister and Cabinet.

Narayan's AI strategy brief and the AI Security Institute now sit in the Cabinet Office. In practice, DSIT's former portfolio — which included the AI Opportunities Action Plan, the AI Security Institute, the £500m Sovereign AI Fund, and UK Research and Innovation — now sits across more than one department.

| Function | Old Home (DSIT) | New Home |
|---|---|---|
| AI Security Institute | DSIT | Cabinet Office / OPMC |
| Science & research (UKRI) | DSIT | DBIST |
| AI strategy | DSIT | Cabinet Office |
| Digital transformation | DSIT | DCMS |
| Senior AI minister | Junior, DSIT | Cabinet, cross-cutting |

## The Tension at the Core

A Cabinet seat is real leverage, and Narayan now has it. The open question is whether one minister can direct an agenda whose delivery machinery — compute, the safety institute, research funding, digital regulation — has just been distributed across several departments with competing priorities.

Industry reaction was pointed. "The abolition of DSIT is clearly a bad thing but if anyone could make up for it in Cabinet it would be Kanishka," said Dom Hallas of the Startup Coalition to the BBC. Former government AI adviser Matt Clifford called the move "a big mistake," while AI Security Institute chair Ian Hogarth warned that reorganizations drive out the best civil servants. techUK and the Startup Coalition jointly described it as "the wrong change at the wrong time."

Cabinet Office minister Baroness Anderson pushed back, arguing that science and AI "should no longer sit inside a standalone department but be embedded across Whitehall."

## Historical Parallel: The "Embedded Across Whitehall" Trap

This framing has a troubling precedent. DSIT was itself created in February 2023 as the answer to BEIS fragmentation — when BEIS was dissolved into the Department for Business and Trade, the Department for Energy Security and Net Zero, and DSIT. Now DSIT is being fragmented in turn. The pattern — create specialist department, declare it siloed, dissolve it into broader portfolios, repeat — suggests a structural habit of cycling between concentration and dispersion without resolving the underlying coordination problem.

France's experience is instructive. France identified fragmented AI governance as a major weakness, noting that more than twenty actors were involved in coordination in its first phase, with incomplete budgetary monitoring — leading its Cour des comptes to recommend a dedicated General Secretariat for AI attached directly to the Prime Minister. The UK has landed on something structurally similar to what France concluded it needed, but without codifying the interministerial budget authority.

## The EU Act Complication

The timing compounds the risk. The EU AI Act's full high-risk regime applies from August 2, 2026, and catches UK businesses placing AI systems on the EU market — a substantial proportion of UK SaaS, data-services and consultancy businesses. As we covered in the [August 2 EU Act enforcement piece](https://minwu-ai.github.io/council-vote-this-week-closes-the-legislative-loop-august-2-/), the UK's principles-based divergence from the EU framework creates a dual-compliance burden for UK firms operating across both markets.

A Cabinet-level AI minister is, in theory, better positioned to negotiate mutual recognition arrangements with Brussels than a junior minister in a single department. But that potential is undercut if the institutional machinery needed to execute any such arrangement — UKRI, the AI Security Institute, sector regulators — is scattered across three departments during precisely the window when the EU is standing up its enforcement apparatus.

## The Broader Governance Pattern

This connects to a trend visible across jurisdictions: AI governance is migrating from specialist technical bodies toward political centers. As we noted in the [Colorado governance retreat](https://minwu-ai.github.io/colorado-s-ai-governance-retreat-didn-t-end-the-story-it-cha/) and [FTC preemption posts](https://minwu-ai.github.io/the-ftc-s-ai-accuracy-statement-is-a-federal-preemption-weap/), frontier AI questions are increasingly settled by political actors rather than specialist regulators. The UK move is the most explicit structural instantiation of that trend yet.

> **The structural bet Burnham is making:** political centrality can compensate for institutional dispersion. History suggests the opposite — that dispersed delivery without a coordinating authority produces slower decisions, not faster ones.

## What to Watch

**AI Security Institute independence**: Now housed in the Cabinet Office alongside a political AI Taskforce, watch whether the AISI's published pre-deployment evaluation findings — previously a signal of its relative independence — become more muted or more muscular. That signal will arrive faster than any formal governance review.
