## 🗓️ The Calendar Is Not What Most Teams Think It Is

The Omnibus moved one major date and left others completely intact:

| Obligation | Original deadline | Omnibus outcome |
|---|---|---|
| Article 50 chatbot/AI-disclosure (Art. 50(1),(3),(4)) | Aug 2, 2026 | **Unchanged** |
| Article 50(2) watermarking — new systems | Aug 2, 2026 | **Unchanged** |
| Article 50(2) watermarking — systems already on market | Aug 2, 2026 | Dec 2, 2026 (grace period) |
| Annex III standalone high-risk AI | Aug 2, 2026 | Dec 2, 2027 |
| Annex I embedded/product high-risk AI | Aug 2, 2027 | Aug 2, 2028 |
| New: nudifier/CSAM prohibition (Art. 5) | Not in original Act | Dec 2, 2026 |

August 2, 2026 remains a live compliance date. The Article 50 transparency obligations are largely unaffected by the Omnibus. This point is confirmed across [Gibson Dunn](https://www.gibsondunn.com/eu-ai-act-omnibus-agreement-postponed-high-risk-deadlines-and-other-key-changes/), [White & Case](https://www.whitecase.com/insight-alert/eu-agrees-digital-omnibus-deal-simplify-ai-rules), and [Latham & Watkins](https://www.lw.com/en/insights/ai-act-update-eu-resolves-to-change-rules-and-extend-deadlines) — they agree on the substance, differing only in emphasis.

## The Watermarking Date Is Not One Date — It's Two

The grandfathering rule is narrower than most coverage suggests. AI systems placed on the market before August 2, 2026 benefit from a four-month grace period until December 2, 2026 before the watermarking obligation under Article 50(2) applies. The broader Article 50 transparency obligations — including disclosure requirements when users interact with AI — remain unaffected and proceed from August 2, 2026.

The grandfathering covers only Article 50(2); the other three transparency duties under Article 50(1), (3), and (4) apply from August 2 without any transition. Compliance teams that read "the watermarking deadline moved to December" as applying to their entire Article 50 program have misread the text.

The deferral of the high-risk regime does not translate into breathing room for a generative-AI provider or a marketing team publishing synthetic media: the obligation most likely to touch a mainstream business, and the penalty that backs it, are both on the unmoved part of the calendar.

## The Addition Nobody Planned For

The Omnibus did not only defer — it tightened. Two categories are added to Article 5's prohibited practices, effective December 2, 2026: AI systems that generate or manipulate sexually explicit or intimate images without explicit consent, and AI systems used to generate child sexual abuse material.

This provision was not in the Commission's original November 2025 proposal. Both Council and Parliament pushed it through during trilogue; the [EU Council's own press release](https://www.consilium.europa.eu/en/press/press-releases/2026/05/07/artificial-intelligence-council-and-parliament-agree-to-simplify-and-streamline-rules/) explicitly highlights it as a co-legislator addition.

The exposure is not marginal. Violations may trigger fines of up to €35 million or 7% of annual worldwide turnover, whichever is higher. For providers of general-purpose image- or video-generation tools, the prohibition extends to systems where such generation is a reasonably foreseeable and reproducible outcome — active misuse-risk assessment at the design and deployment stage is now required.

## Historical Parallel: The GDPR Headline Effect, Redux

The "EU delayed the AI Act" narrative is structurally identical to the 2018 GDPR coverage that framed last-minute guidance extensions as general relief. Organizations that stood down GDPR work on the basis of supervisory authority delays found themselves exposed on core obligations that had never moved.

The same misreading is travelling through compliance teams now. Boards heard "delayed" and assumed the entire high-risk machine was pushed back. Article 50 is not a high-risk obligation — it is a separate transparency layer that applies regardless of risk classification, and the Omnibus deliberately left it alone. This builds directly on the labelling Code of Practice analysis at [Europe's AI Labelling Clock Is Ticking](https://minwu-ai.github.io/europe-s-ai-labelling-clock-is-ticking-what-the-final-conten/) — the Code was finalized in June precisely because August 2 enforcement was never in doubt.

## What to Watch

The legislative act enters into force on the third day after Official Journal publication. Every day that passes compresses the runway for teams still building Article 50 infrastructure.

**My read:** The Commission has signaled it treats August 2 transparency enforcement as a credibility test for the entire AI Act regime — having explicitly confirmed that the transparency rules will come into effect in August 2026. The split-deadline structure is not a technicality. It is the story.
