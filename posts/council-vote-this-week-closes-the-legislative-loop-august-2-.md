## The legislative marathon is nearly over

On 16 June 2026, the European Parliament approved the Digital Omnibus on AI with 423 votes in favour, 57 against, and 174 abstentions. The EU Council is expected to formally adopt the text on 29 June — the last procedural step before Official Journal publication puts the amended AI Act into force.

That Council vote is not a formality. The deferred high-risk dates apply *only if* the Omnibus enters into force before 2 August 2026, when current high-risk rules would otherwise take effect. Miss the window, and companies face the original, undiluted Annex III obligations from August 2.

## What the Final Deal Actually Locks In

The Omnibus restructures, not dismantles, the AI Act. The key dates, confirmed across multiple legal analyses:

| Obligation | Previous deadline | Amended deadline |
|---|---|---|
| Article 50 transparency (most) | 2 Aug 2026 | **2 Aug 2026 — unchanged** |
| Article 50(2) watermarking | 2 Aug 2026 | 2 Dec 2026 |
| Annex III high-risk (standalone) | 2 Aug 2026 | 2 Dec 2027 |
| Annex I high-risk (embedded products) | 2 Aug 2027 | 2 Aug 2028 |
| NCII/CSAM prohibition (new) | — | 2 Dec 2026 |

August 2 remains an active compliance date. Article 50 transparency obligations largely remain on the original schedule.

## August 2 Is Live — What Activates

August 2, 2026 is the date the Commission gains penalty enforcement powers over GPAI providers, Article 50 transparency obligations activate for most AI systems, and national market surveillance authorities can fully investigate and sanction breaches. Penalty tiers are unchanged: €35M/7% of global turnover for prohibited practices, €15M/3% for high-risk and transparency violations, €7.5M/1.5% for misleading information to authorities.

This follows directly from what we covered in [Europe's AI Labelling Clock Is Ticking](/europe-s-ai-labelling-clock-is-ticking-what-the-final-conten/). The Council vote this week removes residual ambiguity: compliance teams now have a single, fixed calendar. For organisations already navigating agentic deployments — where [the governance gap is already wide](/agentic-ai-and-the-governance-gap/) — Article 50 deployer disclosure applies to any AI-facing customer or worker interface. August 2 is not a future problem.

## The Classification Consultation: A Hidden Signal

The Commission has extended its high-risk classification consultation deadline to 23 July 2026; final guidelines will be adopted by end of 2026.

The extension matters more than it looks. The draft guidelines interpret the high-risk conformity assessment test broadly, capturing more AI systems than businesses may have assumed. Providers must exercise care in publicly available materials — instructions for use, technical documentation, marketing — because those descriptions can pull a system into the high-risk track without a deliberate classification decision.

> **The analytic risk here is scope creep by documentation.** Companies that write broad capability claims into product materials may inadvertently self-classify as high-risk. July 23 is the last formal window to push back before the Commission finalises its interpretive framework.

## The Diminishing-Support Pattern

One detail the headlines underplay: political consensus is narrowing. On 26 March 2026, Parliament adopted its negotiating position with 569 votes in favour; on 16 June, the agreed text recorded 423 in favour, 57 against, and 174 abstentions — a near-quadrupling of abstentions.

This signals real internal critics. Civil society has accused EU lawmakers of using the fast-track Omnibus process to weaken digital rights protections. This tension will likely resurface at the high-risk enforcement stage in 2027, when the Commission must certify that harmonised standards are available before the December clock starts.

This governance fragility has a clear parallel: GDPR's Article 29 working party guidance repeatedly lagged legal deadlines, creating enforcement gaps that persisted for years. The AI Act's classification guidance is now on the same trajectory — final guidelines due end-2026, obligations live by December 2027. Eighteen months is enough time to act; it is not enough time to wait.

## What to Watch

The Council will adopt on June 29 as signalled. The real watch item is whether the Commission's high-risk classification guidelines — finalised post-July 23 consultation — define the Article 6(3) exception narrowly or permissively. A narrow reading compresses the relief the Omnibus appears to offer. Compliance teams building their inventory now, before final guidance lands, are making the right call.
