Anthropic's June 4 paper, "When AI Builds Itself," is the most data-heavy safety intervention a frontier AI lab has published to date. It also arrives at the most commercially fraught moment in the company's five-year history.

## 📊 The Numbers That Anchor the Argument

By May 2026, more than 80% of the code landing in Anthropic's repository is no longer written by a human—it is written by Claude. The figure comes from the essay by Marina Favaro and Jack Clark, titled *When AI builds itself*, published by the Anthropic Institute.

The paper notes an important nuance in the measurement: Anthropic leadership have publicly estimated that 90% or more of their code is written by Claude, including scripts and experimental code; the >80% figure specifically measures the share of lines merged to production attributable to Claude—a more conservative methodology.

Productivity effects are equally striking. In Q2 2026, the typical engineer was merging 8× as much code per day as in 2024, because much of the code is written by Claude, with the engineer directing and reviewing rather than typing it themselves.

On autonomous capability, Anthropic's internal data aligns with a broader pattern tracked by METR, a non-profit that benchmarks AI capabilities. The length of tasks AI can reliably complete on its own has been doubling roughly every four months, accelerating from an earlier pace of every seven months. In March 2024, Claude Opus 3 could handle tasks that take a human about four minutes; by early 2025 that extended to hour-and-a-half tasks; today, Claude Opus 4.6 handles 12-hour tasks, and METR found that Mythos Preview could sustain work for at least 16 hours.

## The RSI Threshold—and Why It Matters for Governance

Recursive self-improvement (RSI) is the scenario in which an AI system becomes capable of autonomously designing and training its own successor—a model more powerful than itself—triggering a cycle of improvements that feeds itself and accelerates without a human having to intervene at every step. Anthropic states this has not happened and is "not inevitable," but warns it "could come sooner than most institutions are prepared for."

Co-founder Jack Clark put the probability of recursive self-improvement at 60% by end of 2028. Not everyone accepts that timeline: AI researcher Gary Marcus argues the odds of human-free recursive self-improvement by 2028 are below 10%, with a more realistic path stretching into the 2030s.

## The Policy Proposal—and Its Nuclear Analogy

Researchers Marina Favaro and Jack Clark argued that the world should have the option to slow or temporarily pause frontier AI development so that "societal structures and alignment research" can keep up, tying the argument to the RSI threshold.

> *"Without a global coordination mechanism, companies and governments will have to make difficult decisions about safety while under competitive and geopolitical pressures."* — Anthropic, June 4, 2026

Anthropic compared the verification challenge to Cold War nuclear arms control, and acknowledged it would be harder. Unlike nuclear development, AI training cannot be detected by satellites like missile silos, and the only material required is compute, which is available everywhere. Crucially, the paper calls for the world to have the option of a coordinated slowdown or temporary pause—explicitly not a unilateral stop by Anthropic, which they say would simply change who leads rather than create the necessary safety conditions.

## The Pushback

The proposal landed in hostile political terrain.

| Critic | Core Objection |
|---|---|
| David Sacks (White House AI adviser) | Regulatory capture; harms open-source competitors |
| U.S. officials | Any slowdown cedes ground to China |
| Gary Marcus (researcher) | RSI-by-2028 probability is below 10% |

David Sacks, an informal adviser to President Trump, accused Anthropic of running a "regulatory capture agenda" that would ban lower-cost open-source models; U.S. officials argue any slowdown risks handing China a decisive strategic edge.

Analyst Holger Mueller of Constellation Research notes that a development freeze would primarily benefit front-runners by locking in the status quo, allowing Anthropic to maintain and expand its market share in the B2B sector.

## The IPO Shadow

The conflict-of-interest question is unavoidable. Anthropic filed confidentially for an IPO on June 1, 2026—just three days before the paper's publication—following a $65 billion Series H that pushed the company's valuation close to $1 trillion.

While Anthropic has long presented itself as more safety-conscious than other major AI labs, the timing—just before what could be one of the largest tech IPOs in history—has some observers questioning whether it is also a way to stir up hype before the company's public debut.

For risk and governance practitioners, the paper's substantive data deserves to be evaluated independently of its timing. The code-authorship figures are auditable in principle; the task-horizon trend is externally corroborated by METR. Whether or not one accepts the RSI timeline, the operational reality—AI systems directing their own development at scale, with human review rather than human authorship as the primary control—already demands updated model governance frameworks.
