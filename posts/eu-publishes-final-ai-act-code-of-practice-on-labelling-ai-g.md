The European Commission has handed the AI industry its most operationally detailed instruction yet on content provenance — and the clock is running. The final Code of Practice on marking and labelling of AI-generated content was published on June 10, arriving roughly seven weeks before Article 50 transparency obligations begin applying on August 2, 2026.

> **The Code is voluntary. The obligations it helps you meet are not.** — Enterprise DNA, summarising the compliance dynamic precisely.

## What Article 50 Actually Requires

The AI Act establishes a horizontal set of transparency obligations aimed at mitigating risks of deception, manipulation, and misinformation arising from generative AI. Article 50 requires that outputs of generative AI systems be identifiable as AI-generated or manipulated, and that users be informed where content constitutes a deepfake or where AI-generated text is published to inform the public on matters of public interest.

Three specific content categories trigger mandatory disclosure:

- **Deepfakes and AI-generated or AI-manipulated text** published on matters of public interest must be clearly labelled.
- **Interactive AI systems** — users must be informed when they are interacting with a system such as a chatbot.

Crucially, transparency obligations are not limited to systems classified as "high-risk": they apply to any AI system used in the four situations Article 50 covers, making it relevant to every business that uses generative AI to produce content.

## How the Code Was Drafted

The code was developed through an extensive multi-stakeholder process launched in September 2025, involving over 187 participants from industry, academia, civil society, rightsholders, and EU Member States represented in the AI Board. Six independent experts, appointed by the AI Office, led the drafting process.

## The Two Primary Technical Mechanisms

The guidance directs AI providers to add metadata using two primary mechanisms: digitally-signed metadata (sub-measure 1.1.1) and imperceptible watermarking (sub-measure 1.1.2). An optional mechanism (sub-measure 1.1.3) is fingerprinting or logging, which requires a registry database.

The Commission's final Code converts Article 50 from statutory text into operational steps: signed metadata, watermarking, free detection tools, and a common set of EU labelling icons.

| Mechanism | Layer | Who It Applies To |
|---|---|---|
| Digitally-signed metadata | Machine-readable provenance | Providers (Section 1) |
| Imperceptible watermarking | Signal embedded in output | Providers (Section 1) |
| Fingerprinting / logging | Optional registry-based | Providers (Section 1) |
| Visible EU icon + disclosure | Human-readable label | Deployers (Section 2) |

## Standardised EU Icons: Reducing Friction

The final code has been streamlined and simplified, providing more flexibility for signatories and reducing the compliance burden, while promoting the use of open standards for AI content marking and a common EU icon for labelling to simplify compliance and reduce costs. The annex includes illustrative examples of a possible common EU icon, to be defined in a dedicated task force and made available to Code signatories, aiming to offer a single visual language recognisable to European users — on the model of food quality marks — so enterprises can rely on a standard graphic element rather than a proprietary choice.

## Penalties and the Grandfathering Rule

Non-compliance with Article 50 exposes providers and deployers to administrative fines of up to €15 million or 3% of total worldwide annual turnover, whichever is higher.

There is one narrow concession for legacy systems: generative AI systems placed on the market or put into service before August 2 must comply with the watermarking requirements only as of December 2, 2026. Chatbot disclosure obligations, however, are not deferred. Critically, the Omnibus political agreement of May 7, 2026 did not postpone Article 50 — a point many enterprise teams appear to have missed.

## What Comes Next Before August 2

The Commission will complement the Code with guidelines on the implementation of the transparency obligations for certain AI systems under Article 50 of the AI Act, to be published ahead of August 2, 2026. The AI Act expressly provides for the development of voluntary codes of practice, which may be relied upon by economic operators to demonstrate compliance.

Non-signatories do not violate the law by declining to sign, but they must demonstrate to competent authorities how they meet the same obligations through their own means — and the drafts explicitly signal that signatories receive "increased trust" from enforcement, while non-signatories face more stringent scrutiny with gap analyses on a case-by-case basis.

## Enterprise Governance Implications

The Code's reach extends well beyond EU-headquartered firms. As the Code converges on interoperability standards and common iconography, organisations that have already embedded these into their products and workflows will find it easier to operate across the EU market — and for AI providers in particular, the Code is increasingly shaping what enterprise customers will expect to see, and require contractually, from their technology partners.

Supply-chain accountability is an immediate action item. Downstream organisations should not assume that transparency obligations sit only with the original AI model provider or only with the final deployer; all organisations across the value chain should have contracts, governance documents, and internal approval processes that clearly allocate responsibilities and dependencies.

Provenance metadata that proves whether content is authentic will become both a compliance artifact and evidence — a consideration that should now be on the agenda of every model risk committee and AI governance board with EU-facing operations.
