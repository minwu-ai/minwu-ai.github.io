## 🏛️ What Was Actually Decided

The European Commission selected EUROPA, a consortium led by Milan-based Domyn, as winner of its Frontier AI Grand Challenge — a project to develop an open-source AI model covering all 24 official EU languages. The winner receives up to 2.5% of EuroHPC computing capacity for one year on AI-optimised supercomputers. The challenge targets models with at least 400 billion parameters, using efficient architectures like Mixture-of-Experts (MoE).

Domyn (formerly iGenius), led by Uljan Sharka, was selected for strategic vision, execution capability, and potential impact. It focuses on "sovereign AI" — enabling organisations to retain control over models, data, and infrastructure — and is already a signatory to the European Code of Practice for General-Purpose AI models, alongside OpenAI, Google, Microsoft, and Mistral AI.

## The Sovereignty Logic — and Its Limits

Dependence on large American and Chinese models raises issues beyond technical efficiency: data security, regulatory control, intellectual property, and digital sovereignty. As Il Sole 24 Ore frames it: a frontier model trained on European soil under European governance means applying the AI Act without negotiating with non-EU entities, and multilingual capability transforms linguistic fragmentation into competitive advantage.

The historical parallel is instructive. Airbus — assembled in the 1970s from French, German, and British aerospace assets — needed roughly a decade and sustained state subsidy before credibly challenging Boeing. EUROPA faces a compressed timeline competing against models backed by private compute budgets an order of magnitude larger.

## ⚖️ The Regulatory Stress Test Nobody Is Talking About

The open-source exemption under the AI Act is partial, not blanket. Free and open-source GPAI models are exempt from most transparency requirements — but must still comply with copyright rules and publish a training data summary. Critically, if a model poses systemic risk (trained above the 10²⁵ FLOPs threshold), all obligations apply in full, including adversarial testing and incident reporting.

A 400B+ parameter model trained on EuroHPC infrastructure for a full year is plausibly in systemic-risk territory. The GPAI Code of Practice's Safety and Security Chapter specifies obligations that currently apply to providers like OpenAI's o3, Anthropic's Claude 4 Opus, and Google's Gemini 2.5 Pro.

The Commission is therefore funding a model that will likely face the same compliance bar it imposes on U.S. competitors — with one structural difference: it directly controls the developer. This creates both an opportunity (to demonstrate by example) and a conflict-of-interest risk (leniency toward a state-funded champion).

> **The governance question practitioners should watch:** Will the AI Office enforce GPAI systemic-risk obligations on EUROPA with the same rigour it applies to non-EU providers — or will political calculus soften the scrutiny?

| Dimension | EUROPA (state-funded, open-source) | U.S. frontier labs (private) |
|---|---|---|
| Compute access | Public EuroHPC, up to 2.5% for 1 year | Private clusters, uncapped |
| AI Act obligation | Full GPAI if systemic-risk threshold met | Same |
| Open-source exemption | Partial (copyright + training summary still apply) | N/A (mostly proprietary) |
| Regulator relationship | Funder = regulator | Arm's-length |

## What to Watch

This connects directly to compliance pressures already mounting on EU governance teams — as covered in our analysis of [Europe's AI Labelling Clock](/europe-s-ai-labelling-clock-is-ticking-what-the-final-conten/). A state-backed model subject to Article 50 marking requirements would be a first.

**My read:** EUROPA will ship a model. Whether it competes at the frontier on capability benchmarks is genuinely uncertain given the compute differential. What is more certain — and more consequential for practitioners — is that the project will produce the EU's first live test case for GPAI systemic-risk compliance by a state-affiliated provider. The AI Office's handling of that question will signal more about EU AI governance credibility than any press release ever could.
