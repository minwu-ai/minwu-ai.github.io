## 🗓️ What the Omnibus Actually Changed — and Didn't

The single most consequential fact about August 2, 2026 is what it does *not* do: it does not create new obligations — it makes existing ones enforceable. That distinction is why it is easy to underestimate. The grace period ends in 13 days, and consequence arrives.

The Digital Omnibus produced a genuine compliance reprieve for high-risk AI. On June 16, 2026, the European Parliament approved the amendments by 423 to 57, pushing high-risk obligations out by 12 to 16 months. But the trade-press framing — "EU delays AI Act" — has damaged planning calendars. The EU's own [regulatory framework page](https://digital-strategy.ec.europa.eu/en/policies/regulatory-framework-ai) is unambiguous: governance rules and GPAI obligations became applicable August 2, 2025; high-risk rules for regulated products are extended to 2028. Two separate tracks; only one was touched.

## ⚖️ Three Enforcement Mechanisms Activate Simultaneously

**GPAI penalty powers.** The European Commission's enforcement powers over GPAI providers — including fines up to 3% of global annual turnover or €15 million — become applicable August 2, 2026. The obligations themselves applied from August 2, 2025. What changes is teeth. Per the [European Commission's guidelines](https://digital-strategy.ec.europa.eu/en/policies/guidelines-gpai-providers), it will enforce compliance including with fines from that date.

**Article 50 transparency obligations.** Article 50 is a horizontal transparency obligation applying to specific AI categories regardless of risk classification. Providers of chatbots and virtual assistants must inform users they are interacting with AI; providers of generative AI systems must mark outputs in machine-readable format detectable as artificially generated. One nuance: Article 50(2) marking for certain synthetic-content systems already on the market before August 2 transitions to December 2, 2026 — but this is not a blanket extension for every Article 50 obligation.

**National market surveillance.** For GPAI, the AI Office holds exclusive competence; for Article 50 breaches, national MSAs across all 27 member states become the enforcement arm.

## 📋 Who Is Most Exposed

The frontier labs are not the primary risk cohort — they have engaged with the AI Office for a year. The most exposed organizations are mid-market companies that fine-tune, rebrand, or build on GPAI models without having asked whether they are, in law, providers. If you are building a RAG pipeline or AI agent on a foundation model, you are likely a "deployer." But if you substantially modify the model through extensive fine-tuning, you could be reclassified as a "provider" with significantly heavier obligations — the AI Office's guidelines define the boundary, but it remains a grey area.

Third-country providers face an additional, often-missed obligation: they must appoint an authorized representative in the Union prior to placing their GPAI model on the EU market, unless released under a free and open-source licence.

## The Code of Practice: Safe Harbor with a Catch

Signing the GPAI Code of Practice is voluntary, but the stakes of declining have shifted. For signatories, the Commission will treat adherence as a mitigating factor when fixing fines. Non-signatories must demonstrate compliance via other means and will typically need to provide more detailed information. Per [Jones Day](https://www.jonesday.com/en/insights/2025/08/eu-ai-act-european-commission-publishes-generalpurpose-ai-code-of-practice) and [Latham & Watkins](https://www.lw.com/en/insights/eu-ai-act-gpai-model-obligations-in-force-and-final-gpai-code-of-practice-in-place), the AI Office will not impose penalties for violations if signatories don't immediately implement the Code entirely — assuming good faith — whilst noting full enforcement from August 2, 2026.

## Historical Parallel and Forward Read

GDPR offers the closest precedent. Like the AI Act, GDPR had obligations that technically applied before enforcement infrastructure was operational — and the first enforcement actions came not against the largest platforms but against mid-tier enterprises whose compliance programs were aspirational rather than operational. The difference: GDPR enforcement ramped over 18+ months. The AI Office has already run a year of cooperative engagement and is [explicitly preparing compliance assessments](https://digital-strategy.ec.europa.eu/en/policies/signatory-taskforce-gpai-code-practice). Expect the first formal GPAI enforcement actions within 6–12 months, targeting mid-market providers — not frontier labs — on documentation and transparency failures.
