## A Federal Framework Enters a Crowded Field

On June 4, 2026, <cite index="4-1,4-2">Representatives Jay Obernolte (R-CA) and Lori Trahan (D-MA) released a discussion draft of the Great American AI Act (GAAIA), bipartisan legislation to create a federal framework for how the United States governs artificial intelligence.</cite> The release is significant in scope: <cite index="27-4">the bill contains four major titles covering Frontier AI Governance, Workforce, Cybersecurity, and Research, Development, and International Cooperation.</cite> <cite index="2-1">The draft builds on recent bipartisan legislation developed under the auspices of the House Bipartisan AI Task Force, of which Representative Obernolte served as co-chair.</cite>

The frontier governance provisions are the most operationally significant. <cite index="55-6">The bill would require developers of frontier AI models to disclose information about those models, obtain a form of third-party audit, and refrain from retaliating against whistleblowers.</cite> <cite index="30-7">It would place binding requirements specifically on "large frontier developers" — companies with more than $500 million in annual revenue that have trained the most powerful AI models.</cite> <cite index="8-13">The discussion draft would also authorize $100 million per fiscal year for a Center for AI Standards and Innovation.</cite>

## The Preemption Fault Line

The bill's most contested provision is its approach to state law. <cite index="29-1">Title I would preempt state laws and rules "specifically regulating the development of" any AI model, where "development" is broadly defined as "acts performed or directed by a developer prior to its deployment."</cite> <cite index="30-3,30-4">The draft would allow states to retain the power to regulate the use of AI systems within their borders — but states would lose the ability to legislate on how those systems are built.</cite> <cite index="32-5">The preemptions would expire after three years, ensuring Congress continues to revise and update the Act as needed.</cite>

Supporters frame this as a practical necessity. As Obernolte and Trahan put it in their Bloomberg Law op-ed, <cite index="2-8,2-9">"Risks created by AI don't stop at state lines. The most advanced systems are built in one state and used in all 50, shaping jobs, consumers, and public safety everywhere."</cite> Industry groups including <cite index="52-2">the Business Software Alliance (BSA) and the Information Technology Industry Council (ITIC)</cite> have backed the draft.

Opposition, however, was swift. <cite index="59-2">The Democratic House Commission on AI and the Innovation Economy, led by Reps. Ted Lieu, Valerie Foushee, and Josh Gottheimer, issued a statement the day of release saying the group couldn't support the bill because it "does not meet the enormity of the moment."</cite> <cite index="51-2,51-3">The AFL-CIO, representing 15 million workers across 63 affiliates, described its position as a "hard no," with AFT President Randi Weingarten and AFA-CWA President Sara Nelson characterizing the bill as "a giveaway to the AI industry and a handful of trillion-dollar companies, at the expense of American workers."</cite> <cite index="57-1">The bill has received "intense" pushback from the civil rights, labor, and AI safety communities</cite> more broadly. Notably, <cite index="51-9">the Senate voted 99-1 to reject a similar preemption provision as recently as July 2025</cite> — a political precedent that will shadow the bill's path forward.

## Colorado: Repeal, Replace, and Relitigate

The GAAIA arrives just as the state regulatory landscape is itself in flux. Colorado's original AI Act — widely cited as the nation's first comprehensive state AI law — has had a turbulent journey. <cite index="35-1">The operative date for SB 24-205 was extended twice, and a court temporarily suspended enforcement in early 2026 following a lawsuit filed by xAI, which the U.S. Department of Justice intervened to support.</cite>

<cite index="35-2,35-3">On May 14, 2026, Colorado adopted a new AI law, SB 26-189, replacing the 2024 law it had passed but never put into practice. The new law forgoes three of the most significant obligations of the prior statute: risk management programs, impact assessments, and the duty to use reasonable care to prevent algorithmic discrimination.</cite> <cite index="35-4">In their place, the law narrows its scope to automated decision-making technologies (ADMT) that make "consequential decisions" and imposes four new operational duties: to notify users when they interact with AI, disclose to consumers within 30 days of an adverse outcome, correct inaccurate personal data when requested, and provide meaningful human review and reconsideration.</cite> <cite index="36-6">SB 26-189 will take effect January 1, 2027, unless delayed by xAI's pending legal challenge.</cite>

The xAI litigation is not resolved. <cite index="41-7">The court stay technically extends to successor legislation, meaning SB 26-189 also cannot be enforced until the injunction question is resolved and rulemaking is complete.</cite> <cite index="41-18">The DOJ's continued involvement signals the federal government intends to challenge state AI laws broadly, not just Colorado's original version.</cite> Compliance teams tracking a "June 30 deadline" should be aware that the original law no longer exists in any form — its replacement carries its own legal and rulemaking uncertainty well into 2027.

## The EU Blinks — But Does Not Retreat

Across the Atlantic, European regulators have bought themselves time without abandoning their ambitions. <cite index="22-7">On May 7, 2026, EU lawmakers reached political agreement on revisions to the AI Act, bringing some much-needed certainty after a fraught end to April, when negotiations on the Digital Omnibus on AI almost broke down.</cite> <cite index="23-1">The provisional agreement introduces fixed timelines for delayed application of high-risk rules: the new application dates are 2 December 2027 for stand-alone high-risk AI systems and 2 August 2028 for high-risk AI systems embedded in regulated products.</cite>

The delay was driven by a structural problem, not a change of intent. <cite index="20-13">The proposal was prompted by the fact that the Act's timely implementation had faced significant delays, particularly around the designation of national competent authorities and the finalization of harmonized standards and compliance tools needed for high-risk AI system requirements.</cite> Critically, <cite index="19-8,19-9,19-10">August 2, 2026 remains a live compliance date: the Article 50 transparency obligations are largely unaffected by the Omnibus, and businesses subject to those obligations should continue preparing for that date.</cite> The message from Brussels is clear: prepare now; do not treat an extended deadline as permission to defer.

## What Enterprises Should Do Now

The three-way dynamic — a draft US federal law, an evolving state landscape, and a deferred but still active EU framework — creates real planning risk. No single standard is in force globally, yet each jurisdiction is generating enforceable obligations on overlapping timescales.

<cite index="44-6">In the absence of congressional action, state legislatures have moved aggressively: more than 1,200 state AI bills were introduced in 2025 alone, with California, Colorado, Texas, Illinois, and New York each enacting substantive measures.</cite> <cite index="45-16,45-17">This fragmented approach creates real challenges: organizations might comply with one state's requirements while unknowingly violating another's.</cite>

For risk and governance practitioners, several immediate priorities follow:

- **Frontier model developers** should treat GAAIA's disclosure, audit, and whistleblower provisions as directional signals regardless of enactment timeline. <cite index="27-5,27-6">The requirements draw heavily from recently enacted frontier model laws in California, New York, and Illinois</cite> — obligations that remain in effect whatever Congress does.
- **Colorado-exposed deployers** should inventory ADMT systems used in consequential decisions now. <cite index="35-8,35-9">The compliance deadline under SB 26-189 is January 1, 2027; the remainder of 2026 should be used to prepare, including inventorying systems and renegotiating vendor terms.</cite>
- **EU-facing organizations** should not treat the December 2027 deadline as a reason to reset their programs. <cite index="21-12,21-13">The foundations worth building before August remain the right starting point: AI inventory, risk classification, governance frameworks, oversight policies, and clear accountability. Understanding which AI systems are in use, which may be high-risk, and what controls are already in place remains essential.</cite>

The GAAIA is still a discussion draft. <cite index="54-3,54-4">It remains a draft with sponsors seeking feedback, and the House Democratic Commission on AI has already framed itself in opposition, leaving the bill's path to formal introduction filled with uncertainty.</cite> Enterprises cannot afford to wait for resolution. The prudent posture is to build governance infrastructure against the most demanding applicable requirements, document jurisdiction-specific obligations separately, and maintain a live regulatory watch across all three theaters simultaneously.
