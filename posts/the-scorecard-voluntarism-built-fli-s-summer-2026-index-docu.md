## 📊 The Scores

The Summer 2026 Index evaluates nine companies across 37 indicators spanning six domains:

| Company | Overall Grade | Change vs. Prior |
|---|---|---|
| Anthropic | C+ | Holds top spot |
| OpenAI | C | Slipped from C+ |
| Google DeepMind | C | Third place |
| Meta | D+ | ↑ Rose from D |
| Z.ai / Alibaba Cloud | D– | — |
| xAI | F | ↓ Fell from fourth |
| DeepSeek | F | — |
| Mistral | F | New entrant, last |

Anthropic held the top spot with a modest C+, while OpenAI slipped from C+ to C. xAI, DeepSeek, and Mistral received failing grades — one company each from the U.S., China, and Europe.

## The Structural Finding That Actually Matters

Behind the letter grades sits a documented behavioral trajectory. Anthropic, OpenAI, Google DeepMind, and Meta have weakened or voided pledges to pause unilaterally if redlines are approached — what reviewers call "moving the goalpost."

[Time's exclusive reporting](https://time.com/7380854/exclusive-anthropic-drops-flagship-safety-pledge/) showed that Anthropic committed in 2023 to never train an AI system unless it could guarantee adequate safety measures in advance, but recently overhauled its RSP, scrapping that core promise. The rationale from Anthropic's chief science officer was candid: "We didn't really feel, with the rapid advance of AI, that it made sense for us to make unilateral commitments … if competitors are blazing ahead."

That logic — *I will stay safe only if rivals do too* — is a coordination failure in its purest form. Each lab can now point to the others to justify its own retreat.

## Existential Safety: A Persistent Floor-Level Failure

Existential Safety is the weakest domain industry-wide; no company exceeds C–. Constructive attempts exist — Anthropic's constitutional classifiers, OpenAI's governance proposals, Google DeepMind's monitoring commitments — but panelists judge them "entirely inadequate."

The panel questions dominant paradigms such as interpretability and Chain-of-Thought monitorability because "detection is not prevention." This directly challenges the technical roadmap [Google DeepMind published last month](https://minwu-ai.github.io/deepmind-s-ai-control-roadmap-from-trust-the-model-to-contai/): the architecture may be sound, but the ambition is insufficient.

The critique also lands on framework quality. As U.S./EU compliance deadlines near, major labs published fuller frameworks — yet they often lack quantitative thresholds, independent audits, and clear decision authority. This maps directly to the SR 26-2 gap analyzed [here](https://minwu-ai.github.io/sr-26-2-s-genai-carve-out-creates-a-structured-governance-gap/): frameworks without hard triggers and external enforcement are disclosures, not controls.

## The Governance Vacuum the Index Makes Visible

> "Companies have backed away from earlier commitments to release new systems only with safety measures appropriate for their capability levels; now, they're planning to release them even if it's demonstrably unsafe to do so." — Prof. Stuart Russell, UC Berkeley, panel member

The historical parallel is the financial industry pre-2008: self-regulatory organizations issued codes, firms signed them, and the codes bent whenever competitive pressure made compliance expensive. The FLI Index functions as early stress-test disclosure — a forcing function that creates a written record against which future conduct can be measured.

One genuine counterpoint: Mistral argues the report penalizes open-source models, since enterprises control fine-tuning and can build context-specific safety controls. That critique has methodological merit — the Index grades on self-governance disclosure, structurally disadvantaging developers who delegate safety downstream. But it illustrates the core problem: when safety is diffused across thousands of deployers, no one is accountable for the aggregate.

## What to Watch

The [METR Frontier Risk Report](https://minwu-ai.github.io/the-insider-threat-you-built-yourself-metr-s-frontier-risk-r/) reached a structurally similar conclusion about internal agents. Two independent bodies, different methodologies, same directional finding.

The most important result in the [FLI's Summer 2026 AI Safety Index](https://futureoflife.org/ai-safety-index-summer-2026/) is not any individual grade — it is the direction of travel: capabilities have accelerated, labs have moved their own goalposts, and no external governance floor has replaced what is being quietly removed. The Index will matter most if it begins influencing procurement and enterprise risk decisions rather than media cycles.
