## 🏫 The Scoreboard

The FLI Safety Index is not a fringe exercise. Scored by [seven independent AI and governance experts across 37 indicators in six domains](https://futureoflife.org/ai-safety-index-summer-2026/), it is one of the few structured, recurring attempts to hold frontier labs to account publicly. The Summer 2026 verdict: not one of nine companies earns a passing grade, and the trajectory is worsening.

| Company | Grade | Movement |
|---|---|---|
| Anthropic | C+ | Stable (led 5/6 domains) |
| OpenAI | C | ↓ (slipped from C+) |
| Google DeepMind | C | Stable |
| Meta | D+ | ↑ (rose from D) |
| Z.ai | D- | — |
| Alibaba Cloud | D- | — |
| xAI / SpaceXAI | F | ↓↓ (fell from 4th to 7th) |
| DeepSeek | F | — |
| Mistral | F | New entrant, last place |

xAI — rebranded as SpaceXAI after merging with Musk's ventures — fell to an F, its decline attributed to weakened safety transparency following SpaceX acquisition.

## The Sharpest Finding: Existential Safety Is Broken

In the report's most damning row — Existential Safety — no company scores above a C-. Every firm was judged "entirely inadequate" at managing existential risks. The CEOs of OpenAI, Google DeepMind, Anthropic, and xAI all signed a 2023 statement warning AI could cause human extinction, yet FLI researcher Sabina Nong noted: "The leading companies are chasing recursive self-improvement at an unprecedented rate… but no company has any concrete idea about how to make sure these models will stay under human control."

This directly amplifies the warning in [METR's Frontier Risk Report](https://minwu-ai.github.io/the-insider-threat-you-built-yourself-metr-s-frontier-risk-r/), which found that internal AI agents at four frontier labs already plausibly had the means and opportunity for rogue behavior — with limited alignment, not robust control, as the current restraint. The FLI Index confirms that policy frameworks intended to complement technical controls are themselves hollowing out.

## 🚩 The Red-Line Retreat

Reviewers said Anthropic, OpenAI, Google DeepMind, and Meta have weakened or eliminated earlier commitments to pause development if systems approached specified danger thresholds — described as "moving the goalposts." From 2024 to 2026, these same companies reversed earlier bans on military applications, joining xAI and Mistral in actively seeking defense partnerships. Anthropic drew criticism for "questionable military engagements," including a reported link to a strike causing mass civilian deaths.

This is not just reputational risk. It is a direct governance failure: the labs most trusted to model responsible behavior are normalizing expansions their own prior safety frameworks prohibited.

## Frameworks Without Teeth

Companies are publishing safety frameworks, but these lack quantitative thresholds, genuinely independent audits, and clear decision authority. This echoes the pattern identified in [DeepMind's AI Control Roadmap](https://minwu-ai.github.io/deepmind-s-ai-control-roadmap-from-trust-the-model-to-contai/) and the [Sonnet 5 system card](https://minwu-ai.github.io/the-sonnet-5-system-card-is-a-master-class-in-what-frontier-/): disclosure breadth is rising; accountability mechanisms are not keeping pace.

A historical parallel is instructive. The 2000s financial rating agency model showed that voluntary, self-funded assessments struggle to resist the competitive pressure they are designed to constrain. The FLI report notes the voluntary safety system has begun eroding before governments have put a durable alternative in place.

> "Companies have backed away from earlier commitments to release new systems only with safety measures appropriate for their capability levels; now, they're planning to release them even if it's demonstrably unsafe to do so." — Prof. Stuart Russell, UC Berkeley

## What to Watch

Mistral pushed back, arguing the framework was ill-suited to open-source development — a legitimate methodological tension, not a dismissal. But the scoring debate is a distraction from the structural signal. As Tegmark noted, the worst scorers come from three different continents, showing "this is a global problem" requiring regulation.

With the [EU AI Act's August 2 enforcement date](https://minwu-ai.github.io/one-vote-left-why-august-2-still-matters-more-than-the-omnibus/) looming and [Trump's voluntary pre-release window](https://minwu-ai.github.io/trump-s-ai-executive-order-builds-the-scaffold-but-won-t-lig/) carrying no binding force, governance scaffolding remains exactly as weak as the Index implies. Expect the Winter 2026 edition to show further erosion on existential safety unless binding thresholds — not self-published frameworks — enter the picture.
